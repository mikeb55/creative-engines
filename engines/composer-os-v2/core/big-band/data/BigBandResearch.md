## SECTION 1 — CORE COMPOSERS (BEHAVIOUR)

### Duke Ellington

#### 1. Form / section design

- Intros use unusual colors (muted brass, clarinet, piano), short motives, and counterpoint rather than stock drum vamps. [web:32][web:34][web:39]
- Melody statements often: sax lead + brass/trombone counter‑lines, not a single naked tune with pads. [web:32][web:34]
- Sections feel episodic but motive‑linked: new colors/registrations for the same cells across the chart. [web:34][web:39]
- Soli often treat sections as small orchestras with internal counterpoint and non‑parallel motion. [web:32][web:34]
- Backgrounds are tailored to soloist color, often through‑composed rather than looped riffs. [web:34][web:43]
- Shout‑like passages usually transform head material instead of introducing a new “anthem riff.” [web:34][web:43]
- Endings often use reharmonised tags, coloristic fades, or soloist‑specific codas instead of stock tutti punches. [web:32][web:39]

#### 2. Density behaviour

- Prefers texture‑driven density: multiple contrapuntal lines vs sheer number of players. [web:32][web:34]
- Local waves: thin textures (piano + soloist) → sudden multi‑line ensemble → back to thin. [web:34][web:39]
- Increases density by adding counter‑lines and inner voices, not just doubling the lead. [web:33][web:39]
- Often maintains clear registral separation even in dense moments so lines remain distinct. [web:33]

#### 3. Section identity

- Saxes: individualised, frequent clarinet lead, strong use of inner counterlines and obbligatos. [web:32][web:39]
- Trumpets: mutes, growls, plunger effects; often short bursts, fanfares, coloristic hits. [web:32][web:39]
- Trombones: melodic counter‑voices, response phrases, low pads with character. [web:39]
- Rhythm: piano as color/orchestrator, bass and drums flexible around the beat, dynamic interaction with horns. [web:32][web:39]

#### 4. Riff / motif handling

- Short motives recur with reharmonisation, inversion, augmentation/diminution. [web:34][web:39]
- Multi‑riff textures: 2–3 distinct lines in different ranges interlock rhythmically. [web:34]
- Motifs tied to players (e.g. specific alto shapes) to “characterise” textures. [web:34][web:39]

#### 5. Rhythm / swing behaviour

- Swing is elastic; tempo centre stable but phrasing pushes/pulls around it. [web:32][web:39]
- Hits follow melodic contour and phrase shape rather than stock set‑piece patterns. [web:34]
- Background rhythms often cross phrase boundaries, long lines over multiple bars. [web:34]

#### 6. Orchestration / voicing behaviour

- Multifocal textures: several simultaneous focal lines in different registers. [web:33][web:39]
- Voicings allow parallelism, quartal colors, and altered dominants as timbre, not just function. [web:33]
- Signature color combinations (clarinet + muted brass, low bone + bari, etc.). [web:32][web:39]

#### 7. Shout chorus behaviour

- Shouts = intensified head motives, often with counter‑shout textures between sections. [web:34][web:43]
- Not always full‑band homophony; different subsections may have different lines/accents. [web:34][web:43]
- Rhythmic tension built by extending phrases and delaying cadences. [web:34]

#### 8. ENGINE RULES (Ellington)

- Use ≥2 independent melodic lines whenever density > “thin”.
- When thickening texture, first add new contrapuntal lines before adding doublings.
- For intros, prioritise small ensembles with unusual color combos; avoid full‑band hits.
- For melody statements, always give at least one other section a countermelody or obbligato.
- Tag motives with IDs and require their transformed reappearance in later sections.
- Allow non‑functional/altered chords and parallel sonorities as valid voicing targets.
- In shouts, derive lead line from earlier motives (interval pattern/rhythm) instead of generating a brand‑new riff.
- Enable counter‑shout mode: different sections may carry distinct rhythmic cells simultaneously.
- Rhythm section timing variance allowed ± small offsets to simulate elastic time.
- Use player‑linked motif palettes (e.g. “alto 1 vocabulary”) when assigning lines.
- Prefer endings that reuse intro or head material in altered color/harmony.

### Count Basie

#### 1. Form / section design

- Default form: intro → head (riff‑based) → solos with backgrounds → shout → head/tag. [web:38][web:42]
- Intros: short, groove‑oriented; piano figure + rhythm section, sometimes a simple horn riff. [web:42]
- Solos framed by simple, repetitive backgrounds that enter/exit to shape intensity. [web:38][web:42]
- Shout is clearly separate and climactic, often a “super‑riff” based on earlier material. [web:35][web:38]
- Endings: short tags, band buttons, or Basie piano fill + final hit. [web:42]

#### 2. Density behaviour

- Baseline texture is sparse: rhythm section + single riff or head line. [web:42]
- Density increases by stacking riffs: sax riff → add brass riff → add trombone pads. [web:38][web:42]
- Uses clear block‑based changes at chorus boundaries or new soloists. [web:38]
- Avoids sustained dense inner counterpoint; complexity lives in groove and phrasing. [web:42]

#### 3. Section identity

- Rhythm: rock‑solid time; guitar/bass/drums keep four‑to‑the‑bar swing, piano sparse fills. [web:42]
- Saxes: principal riff carriers and warm pads; frequent unisons/soli. [web:42]
- Trumpets: main punch/hit function, high lead power. [web:35]
- Trombones: middle‑density padding, response riffs, and support to sax/brass. [web:35][web:42]

#### 4. Riff / motif handling

- Short repeated riffs are central; charts may be mostly riff‑constructed. [web:42]
- Head and backgrounds built from simple, memorable 1–2 bar cells. [web:38][web:42]
- Layering of riffs at different registers for intensity; limited motivic transformation. [web:38]

#### 5. Rhythm / swing behaviour

- Straight‑ahead, relaxed but clear swing pulse; no heavy metric ambiguity. [web:42]
- Horn hits align with drum kicks; backgrounds tightly locked to ride/hi‑hat feel. [web:42]
- Syncopation is catchy but not overly dense; plenty of silence between figures. [web:42]

#### 6. Orchestration / voicing behaviour

- Functional, clear voicings; diatonic extensions, straightforward spread/close in sections. [web:35]
- Strong dynamic contrast between soft riffs and full shouts. [web:42]
- Sections generally kept “pure” (sax block, bone block, trumpet block) except in shouts. [web:35][web:42]

#### 7. Shout chorus behaviour

- Central feature; loud, riff‑driven, usually homophonic or simple call/response. [web:35][web:38]
- Often restates earlier riffs in higher register and with fuller orchestration. [web:35][web:38]
- Drum set‑ups between phrases; rhythm section still maintains groove. [web:35]

#### 8. ENGINE RULES (Basie)

- Force at least one simple 1–2 bar riff that recurs in head and/or backgrounds.
- Baseline arrangement state = low density: one horn line + rhythm section.
- Implement density changes by adding/removing whole riffs from sections.
- Restrict harmonic vocabulary to mostly functional progressions and clear extensions.
- Disable complex cross‑meter patterns in horns; keep accents on or near strong beats.
- Solos use background riffs that loop with occasional orchestrated changes, not through‑composed lines.
- Shout chorus must reuse or combine earlier riffs and employ full‑band homophony most of the time.
- Rhythm section groove remains active almost continuously; breaks only as short, high‑impact events.
- Trumpet section often carries highest line in shouts; enforce lead‑trumpet‑top voicing.
- Use short chart lengths and clear form boundaries; few ambiguous transitions.

### Thad Jones

#### 1. Form / section design

- Extended and flexible forms: AABA with added interludes, metric changes, and tags. [web:36][web:38]
- Intros can be harmonically rich, sometimes foreshadowing shout or main motives. [web:36]
- Melody statements highly orchestrated from the start (not “thin” heads). [web:36]
- Solos accompanied by evolving backgrounds: none → pads → rhythmic figures → near‑shout. [web:36][web:38]
- Shouts built with clear developmental logic (sequences, side‑steps, modulations). [web:36][web:35]

#### 2. Density behaviour

- Uses planned waves of intensity over larger spans, not only riff stacks. [web:36]
- Within a given density, inner parts are busy and voice‑led. [web:36]
- Contrast via sudden drops (e.g. solo + rhythm only) before rebuilding to climax. [web:36]

#### 3. Section identity

- Saxes: intricate soli, chromatic lines, detailed voice‑leading. [web:36]
- Trumpets: bright, often high; lead defines style; strong role in rhythmic kicks. [web:44]
- Trombones: active inner voices, important counter‑melodies and sub‑features. [web:36][web:44]
- Rhythm: modern swing feel; comp/rhythmic patterns closely interlock with horn writing. [web:36]

#### 4. Riff / motif handling

- Motives systematically varied: sequence, inversion, rhythmic shifts. [web:36]
- Backgrounds derived from motivic cells rather than generic swing clichés. [web:36]
- Multiple motives layered across sections with tight harmonic logic. [web:44]

#### 5. Rhythm / swing behaviour

- Tight swing with intricate syncopation, anticipations, and off‑beat hits. [web:36]
- Frequent metric tension patterns (3‑over‑4, hemiolas) near climaxes. [web:36]
- Drum kicks often notated to interlock with written horn figures. [web:36][web:44]

#### 6. Orchestration / voicing behaviour

- Rich, often close‑to‑medium voicings, integrated tensions, strong bass‑melody relationship. [web:44]
- Cross‑section voicing: lines split across trumpets/bones/saxes, careful register planning. [web:36]
- Uses poly‑chords and modern harmonic stacks for color. [web:36]

#### 7. Shout chorus behaviour

- Shout is a major structural climax with motivic development and harmonic movement. [web:36][web:35]
- Internal lines move actively; not just vertical blocks. [web:44]
- May include modulations or extended turnarounds; often multi‑phrase architecture. [web:36]

#### 8. ENGINE RULES (Thad Jones)

- Allow extended form templates (AABA + interlude, choir features, codas).
- Generate multi‑stage solo background plans (0 → pad → rhythmic → near‑shout).
- Enforce strong voice‑leading: inner parts move stepwise or in small intervals between chords.
- Build motivic transformation functions (sequence, invert, offset rhythm) and reuse across sections.
- Enable cross‑section voicing graphs (assign each chord tone to different section/voice).
- Use metric‑tension patterns near climaxes; parameter for “3‑over‑4 density.”
- Require at least one formal modulation/side‑step in high‑intensity areas.
- Shouts must include sequential motives and moving inner voices; avoid static block hits.
- Rhythm section comp patterns should follow horn rhythms more closely than in Basie mode.
- Permit higher tension densities (altered dominants, upper structures) as defaults.

### Maria Schneider

#### 1. Form / section design

- Often through‑composed or large‑scale multi‑section forms, not simple 32‑bar loops. [web:37][web:41]
- Long, atmospheric intros; texture and harmony evolve before groove is set. [web:41][web:45]
- Melodies emerge organically from texture; “head” may be diffuse. [web:37][web:41]
- Solos embedded in evolving textures; background changes are gradual rather than looped. [web:37][web:41]
- Endings tend to dissolve, thin out, or echo transformed intro material. [web:41][web:45]

#### 2. Density behaviour

- Large‑scale density envelopes: long crescendos/decrescendos across many phrases. [web:41][web:45]
- Relies on textural density (color layers, sustained fields) more than riff layers. [web:37]
- Even at constant dynamic, orchestration (doubles, registers) shifts to maintain motion. [web:41]

#### 3. Section identity

- Saxes: heavy use of flutes/clarinets doubles; strong coloristic role. [web:41][web:45]
- Brass: both pads and bursts, with varied mutes and extended techniques. [web:37][web:45]
- Rhythm: moves between groove engine and orchestral color (e.g. cymbal textures, pedal points). [web:41][web:45]

#### 4. Riff / motif handling

- Short riffs are rare; instead, motives are long, lyrical cells reused in different harmonies and orchestrations. [web:37]
- Motifs often tied to intervallic shapes and contour; development via reharm, register, and color changes. [web:37][web:41]
- Uses ostinati more than riffs: repeating patterns under floating lines. [web:37]

#### 5. Rhythm / swing behaviour

- Uses swing, straight‑8, Latin, folk, and hybrids within single works. [web:41][web:45]
- Rhythmic identity often from ostinati + long melodic phrases that blur barlines. [web:37]
- Cross‑meter phrasing and polymetric feels are common. [web:41]

#### 6. Orchestration / voicing behaviour

- Orchestration‑first; harmony, register, and color are tightly linked. [web:41][web:45]
- Transparent but rich voicings: wide spreads, color tones at extremes, careful doubling. [web:37]
- Expanded palette (guitar, accordion, multi‑winds) as integral, not extra. [web:41][web:45]

#### 7. Shout chorus behaviour

- Traditional Basie‑style shout often absent or softened; climaxes are textural swells. [web:41][web:45]
- Peak sections emphasise layered counter‑lines and harmony rather than riff anthems. [web:37][web:41]

#### 8. ENGINE RULES (Schneider)

- Enable through‑composed form graph; avoid mandatory chorus repeats.
- Permit long intro and outro sections with evolving texture and low rhythmic definition.
- Treat motifs as interval‑contour objects reused with varied harmony/registration.
- Implement ostinato layer that can persist under changing top lines.
- Provide sax double instruments and expanded color instruments; make them default in color roles.
- Use large‑scale density curves (e.g. 64‑bar crescendo) controlling orchestration and dynamics.
- Allow groove type changes mid‑piece (swing ↔ straight ↔ hybrid).
- For climaxes, prioritise layered counter‑lines and wide voicings, de‑prioritise riff‑based tutti shouts.
- Rhythm section state machine switches between “time‑keeping” and “orchestral color”.
- Maintain frequent small orchestration changes even when harmony and groove stay stable.

## SECTION 2 — BIG BAND ERAS (CRITICAL)

### Swing Era (Ellington / Basie)

#### 1. Form & structure

- Common forms: 12‑bar blues, 32‑bar song forms (AABA/ABAC), head → solos → shout → tag. [web:38][web:51]
- Clear sectional boundaries, chorus‑oriented structure. [web:51]

#### 2. Density behaviour

- Alternation between full‑band sections and reduced forces (rhythm + solo, sax soli, etc.). [web:51]
- Basie: block riff stacking; Ellington: local textural shifts with counterpoint. [web:32][web:38][web:42]

#### 3. Rhythm & swing characteristics

- Four‑beat walking bass, steady swing ride, clear backbeat emphasis. [web:42][web:51]
- Rhythmic complexity moderate; strong focus on danceable groove. [web:51]

#### 4. Harmonic language

- Predominantly functional harmony: II–V–I, turnarounds, blues changes. [web:51]
- Extensions up to 9, 11, 13, mostly diatonic; alterations used sparingly. [web:51]

#### 5. LINE WRITING

- Melody and soli lines: strong diatonic outline, chord tones on strong beats. [web:56]
- Swing‑era section lines often parallel harmonisations of top line (same contour). [web:56]

#### 6. Section writing behaviour

- Call/response between brass and reeds; riffs traded between sections. [web:51]
- Sax soli as key feature; brass for hits/shouts, bones as glue. [web:51]

#### 7. What makes the era recognisable

- Danceable pulse, simple forms, clearly delineated riffs and shouts. [web:51]
- Limited harmonic density, straightforward voicings, and prominent section identities. [web:51][web:56]

#### 8. ENGINE RULES (Swing Era)

- Use 12‑bar and 32‑bar templates as primary form generators.
- Enforce steady swing groove with walking bass and ride patterns.
- Limit harmonic complexity to mostly functional changes and modest alterations.
- Default line generator: strong chord tones on beats, mainly stepwise between. [web:56]
- Rely on parallel section harmonisation of melody for soli and tutti. [web:56]
- Implement frequent call/response patterns between reeds and brass.
- Use riff‑based construction for heads and backgrounds.
- Place a clear shout section ~2/3 into chart with full‑band activity.
- Use clear chorus‑based density changes: full band ↔ small group.
- Keep cross‑metric and polymetric effects off by default.

### Bebop Big Band (Dizzy Gillespie era)

#### 1. Form & structure

- Forms based on bebop heads: complex 32‑bar tunes, rhythm changes, advanced blues. [web:50][web:55]
- Arrangements often showcase head in tight unison/soli, then small‑group‑style solos. [web:50]

#### 2. Density behaviour

- High local density in lines (fast notes), but flexible ensemble density (full band ↔ combo). [web:50]
- Background figures may be more fragmented and punctuative than sustained. [web:50]

#### 3. Rhythm & swing characteristics

- Faster tempos and more aggressive swing; strong off‑beat accents and syncopation. [web:50][web:55]
- Afro‑Cuban and Latin rhythms mixed in some works (clave‑type structures). [web:50]

#### 4. Harmonic language

- Complex functional harmony: frequent II–V cycles, tritone substitutions, altered dominants. [web:50][web:55]
- Chromatic approach tones and enclosures as core melodic vocabulary. [web:50]

#### 5. LINE WRITING

- Lines: long, angular, fast moving, with chromaticism and arpeggios outlining substitutions. [web:50][web:55]
- Section soli mirror small‑group bebop vocabulary at big‑band scale. [web:50]

#### 6. Section writing behaviour

- Reeds often carry bebop heads and soli; brass punctuate with syncopated hits. [web:51]
- Rhythm section highly interactive, comping with dense syncopation. [web:50]

#### 7. What makes the era recognisable

- Bebop melodic language in big‑band textures, fast tempos, complex changes. [web:50][web:55]
- Combination of traditional big‑band call/response with modern lines and harmonies. [web:51]

#### 8. ENGINE RULES (Bebop Big Band)

- Use complex 32‑bar bebop forms and advanced blues/rhythm‑changes templates.
- Line generator must support fast, chromatic bebop language (enclosures, arpeggios, substitutions).
- Harmonic engine enables tritone subs and heavily altered dominants as defaults.
- Tempo and rhythmic density parameters set higher than Swing Era.
- Section soli lines mirror bebop vocabulary exactly (no simplified riffs).
- Brass backgrounds: syncopated punctuations rather than long riffs.
- Rhythm section comp generator: dense syncopation and interaction with horns.
- Allow Latin/Afro‑Cuban groove overlays on some sections.
- Shout choruses can be extended, virtuosic soli‑like passages.
- Reduce repetitive riff usage; focus on line‑driven architecture.

### Post‑bop / Thad Jones era

#### 1. Form & structure

- Extended forms, longer spans, multi‑section works with interludes and codas. [web:36]
- Strong architectural planning of climaxes and returns. [web:36]

#### 2. Density behaviour

- Carefully shaped waves of density, with active inner parts throughout. [web:36]
- Sudden contrasts between full band and chamber‑like textures. [web:36]

#### 3. Rhythm & swing characteristics

- Modern swing feel with intricate syncopation, cross‑rhythms, and metric tension. [web:36]
- Drums/horns interlock with specific written kicks and figures. [web:36][web:44]

#### 4. Harmonic language

- Rich functional harmony with extended chords, side‑steps, and poly‑chords. [web:36]
- Strong voice‑leading logic between complex harmonies. [web:44]

#### 5. LINE WRITING

- Lines: complex but highly voice‑led, often mixing diatonic, chromatic, and intervallic ideas. [web:36]
- Inner lines are as important as top line; contrapuntal sections appear often. [web:36][web:44]

#### 6. Section writing behaviour

- Frequent split‑section voicing and cross‑section blends. [web:36]
- Backgrounds and shouts feature multiple moving inner voices. [web:44]

#### 7. What makes the era recognisable

- Modern harmonic vocabulary with big‑band power, plus intricate inner motion. [web:36]
- Architected climactic arcs and sophisticated shout structures. [web:36][web:35]

#### 8. ENGINE RULES (Post‑bop / Thad)

- Enable extended form templates with explicit climax locations.
- Harmonic engine supports side‑steps, extended alterations, and poly‑chord objects.
- Voice‑leading algorithm must optimise inner motion quality, not just outer voices.
- Line generator for soli emphasises stepwise motion + chromatic connections.
- Backgrounds evolve across solo choruses (not static loops).
- Cross‑section voicing maps must be available and frequently chosen.
- Metric‑tension rhythm patterns allowed; parameterised by intensity level.
- Shout generation requires sequenced motives and at least one harmonic shift.

### Contemporary orchestral jazz (Schneider / Brookmeyer)

#### 1. Form & structure

- Long‑form, often multi‑movement or through‑composed works. [web:41][web:49][web:54]
- Solo sections integrated into unfolding ensemble textures, not isolated choruses. [web:41][web:57]

#### 2. Density behaviour

- Large‑scale envelopes with multiple peaks and troughs over long times. [web:41][web:54]
- High importance of texture and orchestration in defining density. [web:41][web:52]

#### 3. Rhythm & swing characteristics

- Flexible groove palette: swing, straight, odd meters, and hybrids. [web:41][web:52]
- Ostinati and polyrhythms common; horns sometimes float over fixed rhythmic layers. [web:37][web:54]

#### 4. Harmonic language

- Mix of functional, modal, and non‑functional harmony; frequent pedal points and color fields. [web:49][web:54]
- Extensive use of quartal/quintal harmony and cluster voicings (especially Brookmeyer). [web:49][web:52][web:54]

#### 5. LINE WRITING

- Long, lyrical lines; motivic development via color and harmony more than bebop changes. [web:41][web:52]
- Counterpoint emphasised, especially in Brookmeyer; multiple simultaneous lines. [web:49][web:54]

#### 6. Section writing behaviour

- Blurred section boundaries; multi‑section voicings common. [web:52][web:54]
- Doubles (flutes/clarinets) and auxiliary instruments integral to texture. [web:41][web:52]

#### 7. What makes the era recognisable

- Orchestral sensibility: color and form on par with groove. [web:41][web:52]
- Use of non‑traditional big‑band instruments and extended techniques. [web:41][web:54]

#### 8. ENGINE RULES (Contemporary orchestral jazz)

- Allow arbitrary form graphs with multiple climaxes and returns.
- Density controlled by orchestration/texture parameters more than player count.
- Harmonic engine supports modal centers, non‑functional fields, pedal‑based harmony.
- Enable quartal/quintal and cluster voicing modes as first‑class options.
- Line generator supports long phrases that float over barlines; less bar‑by‑bar symmetry.
- Section identity is soft: cross‑section and mixed‑instrument voicings common.
- Groove engine supports changing meters and groove types within a piece.
- Climax generation prioritises textural swells and layered counterpoint over riff‑shout paradigms.

## SECTION 3 — SWING, BLUES, HARMONY (FOUNDATIONS)

### 1. What actually creates swing in big band writing

- Alignment of horn rhythms with a clear underlying 4‑beat pulse and walking bass. [web:42][web:51]
- Consistent triplet‑based subdivision in rhythm section and horn phrasing. [web:42][web:51]
- Alternation of tension/release via syncopation vs resolution on strong beats. [web:31][web:47]

### 2. Role of rhythm section vs horns

- Rhythm section: maintains time, groove, and basic harmonic framework. [web:42][web:51]
- Horns: create rhythmic foreground (riffs, hits, lines) and shape large‑scale dynamics. [web:51]
- Interaction: drum kicks accent horn figures; comping responds to line density. [web:42][web:36]

### 3. Common mistakes that kill swing

- Over‑notating hits on every subdivision; no space for groove to breathe. [web:31][web:47]
- Overly complex cross‑rhythms at moderate tempos without clear underlying pulse. [web:31]
- Mis‑aligning horn figures so they consistently fight the ride pattern. [web:42]
- Static background riffs that ignore phrase shape and solo contour. [web:38]

### 4. Blues language in big band

- Beyond 12‑bar: blues vocabulary appears over extended forms and reharmonised progressions. [web:51]
- Phrasing: call/response, 4‑bar or 8‑bar question/answer in lines and riffs. [web:51]
- Inflection: bends, falls, smears, and blue notes embedded in horn lines and voicings. [web:51][web:56]

### 5. Harmonic language

- Functional: II–V–I, turnarounds, blues changes; typical of Swing and early Basie/Ellington. [web:51]
- Modal: static or slow‑moving centers, prevalent in later orchestral jazz. [web:49][web:52]
- Non‑functional: color fields, pedal points, and cluster‑based harmony. [web:49][web:52]
- Chord types: 7(9,11,13) with alterations (b9,#9,b5,#5) more prominent in bebop/post‑bop. [web:50][web:36]
- Upper structures: triads over bass chords to encode altered tensions. [web:49]
- Quartal/quintal harmony: common in modern/post‑bop and contemporary orchestral (Brookmeyer/Schneider). [web:49][web:52][web:54]

### ENGINE RULES (Foundations)

1. Always generate a clear 4‑beat grid with walking bass for swing modes (unless explicitly disabled).
2. Quantise horn rhythms to triplet‑based subdivisions for swing feel.
3. Require some horn resolutions on beats 1 and 3 after syncopated figures.
4. Limit simultaneous independent cross‑rhythms at moderate tempos to preserve groove clarity.
5. Enforce space between figures (rests or sustained chords) in rhythmically busy passages.
6. Tag sections as functional/modal/non‑functional; restrict chord types accordingly.
7. For functional sections, prioritise II–V–I patterns and conventional turnarounds.
8. For bebop/post‑bop, enable tritone substitutions and altered dominants with chromatic approach lines.
9. For modal/non‑functional sections, limit chord root movement and use sustained or ostinato bass patterns.
10. Blues mode: inject blue‑note scale degrees and inflection markers (falls, scoops, doits) into lines/voicings.
11. Upper‑structure mode: construct chord voicings from stacked triads mapping to specified tensions.
12. Quartal/quintal mode: allow stacks of 4ths/5ths as valid voicing primitives, especially in modern styles.
13. Ensure call/response patterns in blues sections: generate paired phrases with contrasting contour/register.
14. Prevent constant tutti playing; rhythm section should support, not mirror, every horn rhythm.
15. Use drum kick generator that aligns with major horn hits while leaving some beats unaccented.

## SECTION 4 — SHOUT CHORUS / RIFF / SOLI LOGIC

### 1. What makes a great shout chorus

- Clear climax in form: placed after buildup, with higher density and intensity. [web:31][web:35][web:47]
- Strong, memorable lead line; harmony and orchestration support its clarity. [web:31][web:47]
- Mixture of tension and release via rhythm (set‑ups, delays, extra bars). [web:31][web:47]
- Full‑band participation (traditional) or intentional textural twist (modern). [web:31][web:53]

### 2. How riffs are constructed and layered

- Built from short, rhythmically hooky cells (1–2 bars) aligning with groove. [web:38][web:42]
- Often derived from head motives or blues/scale fragments. [web:38][web:51]
- Layered vertically across sections with register separation and complementary rhythms. [web:38][web:42]

### 3. Call and response systems

- Basic pattern: one section states a figure, another answers with variation. [web:51]
- Can operate across register (high vs low), timbre (brass vs reeds), or density (solo vs band). [web:51]
- Supports blues language and question/answer phrasing. [web:51]

### 4. Soli writing (saxes, brass)

- Based on a single lead line harmonised for a section (four‑ or five‑part). [web:31][web:47][web:56]
- Uses four‑way close, drop‑2, or spread voicings depending on range and style. [web:31][web:47]
- Swing/bebop soli lines follow chord changes with strong voice‑leading and stylistic vocabulary. [web:36][web:50]

---

### ENGINE RULES — Shout chorus (10)

1. Position shout ~60–75% into form unless explicitly overridden. [web:31][web:38]
2. Require higher density (more active parts and dynamic) than preceding section.
3. Generate a distinct shout lead line, often derived from earlier motives or head. [web:31][web:34][web:47]
4. Harmonise lead line with appropriate voicing style (close/spread/modern) chosen by era. [web:31][web:47]
5. Apply rhythmic tension devices: added bars, drum fills, syncopated lead‑ins before key cadences. [web:31][web:47]
6. Ensure clear phrase structure (2‑, 4‑, or 8‑bar units) with cadential points.
7. Provide at least one contrast subsection (e.g. drop to saxes only, then full band). [web:31]
8. Align major horn hits with drum kicks and rhythmic accents.
9. Reuse or transform prior material (riff/head/motif) in at least one shout phrase.
10. For modern modes, allow counter‑shout: distinct lines in multiple sections.

### ENGINE RULES — Riff writing (10)

1. Riff length typically 1–2 bars; enforce small motif windows. [web:38][web:42]
2. Rhythm must lock cleanly to groove grid (swing, Latin, etc.).
3. Use simple, singable intervals (steps, 3rds, 4ths) for classic swing/Basie riffs. [web:42]
4. Generate riffs from underlying chord tones with occasional passing tones.
5. Mark selected riffs as “core” and require reuse in head, backgrounds, and/or shout. [web:38][web:42]
6. Assign riffs to sections with clear register separation to avoid mud.
7. When layering, enforce complementary rhythms (avoid all riffs filling same gaps).
8. Limit number of simultaneous riffs (commonly 2–3 layers maximum).
9. Provide on/off states per section per chorus (riff layering engine).
10. Optionally derive riffs from blues or head motives for stronger unity. [web:51]

### ENGINE RULES — Soli writing (10)

1. Begin from a single lead line (melody or generated soli line). [web:31][web:47][web:56]
2. Harmonise line using section‑appropriate voicing template (e.g. sax five‑part close). [web:56]
3. Maintain contour of lead line in all voices (parallel/near‑parallel motion) unless using advanced styles. [web:56]
4. Respect instrument ranges; perform octave displacements as needed. [web:31][web:46]
5. For swing/bebop soli, ensure line outlines chord changes clearly with passing tones. [web:50]
6. Integrate occasional unisons/octaves for impact.
7. Support both diatonic and chromatic line options depending on era.
8. Rhythm section underneath should maintain groove with light kicks; avoid over‑busy hits.
9. Optionally pass soli between sections (saxes → bones → trumpets).
10. Allow soli material to reappear in shout chorus harmonised for the full band. [web:31]
