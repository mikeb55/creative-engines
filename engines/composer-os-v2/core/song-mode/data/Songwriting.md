## SECTION 1 — CORE SONGWRITERS (BEHAVIOUR)

### Burt Bacharach

#### Melody behaviour
- Asymmetrical phrase lengths, frequent 3‑, 5‑, 7‑bar groupings. [web:73][web:74]
- Stepwise lines with occasional wide leaps used as drama points.
- Motives sequenced and rhythmically displaced, not simple loops.
- Melodies drive meter: bar lengths change to fit phrase, not vice versa. [web:74][web:76]

#### Harmony behaviour
- Frequent non‑diatonic moves, jazz‑influenced extensions and modulations. [web:73][web:77]
- Borrowed chords, secondary dominants, deceptive resolutions.
- Pedal points under changing upper harmony in bridges and transitions.
- Chord rhythm sometimes decoupled from bar lines.

#### Form / structure
- Verses and choruses with irregular lengths; bridges often reframe key/feel. [web:73][web:77]
- Metric changes between sections (4/4 ↔ 3/4, 5/4, 7/8). [web:74]
- Sometimes multiple “hook zones” (verse and chorus both hooky).

#### Rhythm / phrasing
- Irregular bar groupings; mixed meters inside phrases. [web:73][web:74]
- Off‑beat entries and phrase pickups.
- Rhythmic motifs that cross barlines.

#### Lyric–melody relationship
- Melody first; lyrics adapted to fit irregular phrases. [web:76]
- Lyric syllables stretch/contract to preserve melodic contour.

#### Signature traits
- Asymmetrical phrases, sophisticated harmony, mixed meters, melody‑first design. [web:73][web:74][web:77]

#### ENGINE RULES
- Allow non‑4‑bar phrase lengths (3–7 bars) by default in “Bacharach mode”.
- Permit meter changes inside sections when phrase requires extra beats.
- Generate melody first; force lyric engine to fit given rhythm/contour.
- Prefer stepwise motion with occasional expressive leaps on high‑emotion words.
- Use frequent borrowed chords and secondary dominants between diatonic pillars.
- Allow modulations at pre‑chorus/bridge with smooth voice‑leading.
- Decouple harmonic rhythm from bar boundaries; chord changes can fall mid‑bar.
- Allow pedal bass with upper chord movement in bridges.
- Place hooks in both verse and chorus when possible.
- Align phrase endings with cadential chords; don’t force looped symmetry.

### Stevie Wonder

#### Melody behaviour
- Strong, singable motifs with blues/gospel inflections. [web:78]
- Melodic sequences over changing chords.
- Wide range but smooth registral connections.

#### Harmony behaviour
- Rich R&B/jazz harmony with mode mixture and chromatic shifts. [web:78]
- Uses relative minor/major substitutions and parallel minor borrowing. [web:78]
- Slows harmonic rhythm to build tension before key moments, then surprises. [web:78]

#### Form / structure
- Clear verse/chorus, often with strong pre‑chorus lift. [web:78]
- Bridges and middle‑8s that re‑color existing material.

#### Rhythm / phrasing
- Strong groove integration; melodies lock into rhythm‑section patterns. [web:78]
- Syncopated phrases, off‑beat entries, anticipations.

#### Lyric–melody relationship
- Melisma and rhythmic stretching on key emotional words. [web:78]
- Syllable grouping respects groove accents.

#### Signature traits
- Gospel‑inflected melodies, complex yet natural harmony, groove‑integrated phrasing. [web:78]

#### ENGINE RULES
- Use diatonic progressions as base, then inject modal mixture at pre‑chorus/chorus.
- Implement “tension zone” where harmonic rhythm slows before chorus entry.
- Apply relative major/minor swaps (e.g. IV ↔ ii, I ↔ vi).
- Generate melodic motifs that repeat with transposition over different chords.
- Use syncopated melodic rhythms aligned with drum/bass groove.
- Allow melisma on high‑emotion words; extend note across multiple syllables.
- Use minor7, dominant7, and maj7 chords as primary colors.
- Add passing chromatic chords between diatonic anchors (e.g. stepwise root motion).
- Require a pre‑chorus lift section in Wonder mode.
- Use back‑beat stress in lyric rhythm placement.

### The Beatles (Lennon/McCartney as system)

#### Melody behaviour
- Strong, short motifs; clear contour and memorable shapes. [web:79]
- Mix of stepwise motion and targeted leaps on title/hook words.
- Frequent question/answer phrase pairs.

#### Harmony behaviour
- Mostly functional, but heavy use of modal interchange/borrowed chords. [web:79]
- Non‑standard moves (bVII, bVI, ♭III) for color. [web:79]
- Secondary dominants and chromatic bass lines.

#### Form / structure
- Standard pop forms modified with intros/outros, middle‑8s.
- Clear chorus or refrain hook; occasional no‑chorus “refrain line” structure.

#### Rhythm / phrasing
- Mostly simple meters with occasional push/pull syncopation.
- Phrases often four bars but with pickups and anticipations.

#### Lyric–melody relationship
- Title often in chorus/first line of hook.
- Natural speech inflection; stresses match melodic peaks.

#### Signature traits
- Strong hooks, creative yet accessible harmony, compact forms. [web:79]

#### ENGINE RULES
- Use standard 4/4 with 4‑ or 8‑bar phrase defaults; allow pickups.
- Base harmony on I–IV–V with optional bVII, bVI, ♭III and modal interchange.
- Ensure title appears on melodic high point of chorus.
- Use repetitive motifs (2–4 notes) with slight variations.
- Include a contrasting bridge/middle‑8 in many songs.
- Allow descending chromatic bass passing chords.
- Match lyric stresses to chord changes and melodic accents.
- Include at least one harmonic “surprise” per song (borrowed chord or unusual key move).
- Keep melodic range singable (roughly an octave) by default.
- End sections with strong cadences (V–I or bVII–I).

### Joni Mitchell

#### Melody behaviour
- Wide, angular intervals from alternate tuning shapes. [web:80]
- Asymmetrical phrases with conversational contour.
- Motif cells reused in different registers.

#### Harmony behaviour
- Guitar‑driven voicings; “chords of inquiry” with added 2nds/4ths. [web:80]
- Non‑functional progressions based on shapes and color, not standard functions. [web:80]

#### Form / structure
- Often verse‑driven with evolving story; choruses may be subtle refrains.
- Asymmetrical/through‑composed sections.

#### Rhythm / phrasing
- Phrasing close to speech rhythm; meter can feel loose within steady bar grid.
- Lines start/finish mid‑bar, overlapping measures.

#### Lyric–melody relationship
- Lyric is primary; melody bends to fit prosody. [web:80]
- Internal rhymes and long sentences mapped across phrases.

#### Signature traits
- Alt tunings, coloristic chords, conversational, poetic line flow. [web:80]

#### ENGINE RULES
- Generate “shape‑based” harmony: chord dictionaries with added 2/4 and open voicings.
- De‑emphasise V–I cadences; allow stepwise root movement and pedal harmony.
- Allow irregular section lengths and partial refrains rather than big choruses.
- Let lyric prosody drive phrase start/end; align line breaks with semantic units.
- Permit wide melodic intervals; ensure voice‑leading still singable.
- Use recurring melodic cells in different registers/contexts.
- Avoid rigid rhyme‑scheme‑driven form; allow evolving verse narratives.
- Allow meter‑stable but phrase‑loose bars (entries on “and”s, overlapped phrases).
- Use harmonic color shifts (add2, sus, quartal) to mark emotional pivots.
- Tie title line to a recurring melodic gesture, not necessarily a big hook.

### Donald Fagen (Steely Dan)

#### Melody behaviour
- Mostly stepwise/scale‑wise with occasional leaps; jazz‑like contour. [web:81]
- Melodies often sit in mid‑range, rhythmically intricate.

#### Harmony behaviour
- Sophisticated jazz harmony: extended chords, substitutions, non‑functional passages. [web:81]
- Often starts from harmonic framework; progressions as backbone. [web:81]

#### Form / structure
- Pop song shells with complex bridges and harmonic detours.
- Clear chorus or refrain but sometimes understated.

#### Rhythm / phrasing
- Syncopated, groove‑locked phrasing; lots of anticipations.
- Vocals often sit slightly behind the beat.

#### Lyric–melody relationship
- Syllabic placement tightly aligned with groove; sardonic, narrative text. [web:81]
- Phrasing supports subtle emphasis on key words rather than big belting.

#### Signature traits
- Jazz‑inflected harmony, sardonic lyrics, tight grooves, mid‑range melodies. [web:81]

#### ENGINE RULES
- Use 7th, 9th, 13th chords and frequent secondary dominants/substitutions.
- Allow non‑functional harmonic segments with strong internal voice‑leading.
- Generate mid‑range vocal melodies with moderate leaps.
- Align lyric rhythm to groove with syncopation and anticipations.
- Keep choruses more harmonic/timbral than purely melodic “anthemic” if desired.
- Include harmonic “turns” (side‑steps, tritone substitutions) in verses or bridges.
- Avoid big melismas; use mostly syllabic settings.
- Set vocal phrasing slightly behind beat at phrase level (for performance hints).
- Use bass lines that outline complex harmony cleanly.
- Enable subtle internal rhymes and dense imagery in lyrics.

### Bob Dylan

#### Melody behaviour
- Narrow‑range, chant‑like melodies in many songs. [web:82]
- Repetition of simple contour across many lyric lines.

#### Harmony behaviour
- Very simple diatonic progressions (I–IV–V, I–V–vi–IV etc.). [web:82]
- Occasional modal folk influence.

#### Form / structure
- Strophic forms with many verses; choruses or refrain lines optional. [web:82]
- Flexible line counts and lengths within stanzas.

#### Rhythm / phrasing
- Near‑speech rhythm; lines can stretch freely over bars. [web:82]
- Loose alignment to strict meter, especially in later work.

#### Lyric–melody relationship
- Lyrics primary; melody is a delivery vehicle. [web:82]
- Long lines and dense imagery; end‑line rhyme is structural.

#### Signature traits
- Highly text‑driven, loose prosody, simple harmony with long strophic forms. [web:82]

#### ENGINE RULES
- Use simple diatonic progressions with minimal harmonic variation.
- Choose narrow melodic ranges (5–8 notes) with simple contours.
- Allow many verse iterations with evolving lyrics.
- Permit irregular phrase lengths; don’t enforce strict symmetry.
- Align lyric structure around rhyme schemes (e.g. AAAA, ABAB).
- De‑prioritise strong melodic hooks; focus on line flow.
- Support talking‑blues patterns (same progression, varied verse count).
- Keep rhythmic grid simple but allow text to float over barlines.
- Allow refrains instead of full choruses.
- Prioritise semantic density over melodic variation.

### Paul Simon

#### Melody behaviour
- Tuneful but often mildly asymmetrical; mixes folk‑like and pop arches. [web:83]
- Uses motif repetition with subtle rhythmic/harmonic shifts.

#### Harmony behaviour
- Combines folk simplicity with jazz/pop sophistication. [web:83]
- Chooses which of melody/rhythm/harmony is “primary” per song, then contrasts others. [web:83]

#### Form / structure
- Pop song forms with bridges and codas; some extended narrative pieces. [web:83]
- Strong contrast between verse/chorus/bridge.

#### Rhythm / phrasing
- Rhythm‑driven songs (e.g. groove‑first) vs melody‑first songs. [web:83]
- Phrases can shift accents to follow language.

#### Lyric–melody relationship
- Prosody‑aware; stresses match melodic/harmonic emphasis. [web:83]
- Uses imagery and conversational language.

#### Signature traits
- Balance of groove, melody, and harmony depending on “natural inclination” of song. [web:83]

#### ENGINE RULES
- Tag each song idea as “melody‑first”, “rhythm‑first”, or “harmony‑first” and bias generation accordingly. [web:83]
- For rhythm‑first: build groove pattern, then fit melody/lyrics to it.
- For melody‑first: generate strong motif then harmonise and groove it.
- For harmony‑first: design progression with color chords, then derive melody.
- Ensure verse/chorus/bridge contrast at least one primary dimension (melody, rhythm, or harmony).
- Maintain natural speech stress in lyric‑melody mapping.
- Use mixture of simple diatonic and occasional sophisticated chords.
- Allow extended codas or tags with repeated lines.
- Use folk‑like melodic shapes with occasional unexpected intervals.
- Permit world/ethnic groove patterns underneath pop forms.

## SECTION 2 — SONGWRITING SYSTEM AUTHORS (META-RULES)

### Jimmy Webb (based on *Tunesmith*-style principles) [web:58][web:61][web:65]

#### Core principles
- Strong architecture, clear emotional arc, and craftsmanship over inspiration.

#### Melody rules
- Use motifs and sequences; avoid purely random lines.
- Build climactic high point later in song.

#### Harmony / structure rules
- Support melody emotionally with progressions; use modulations.
- Careful control of key centers and bridges.

#### Lyric/prosody rules
- Lyrics must fit natural speech and melodic contour.
- Use vivid images, avoid clichés.

#### Practical techniques
- Outline song form before writing.
- Develop hooks from small ideas.

#### ENGINE RULES (Jimmy Webb)
- Require motif identification and reuse across sections.
- Place melodic and dynamic climax in later third of song.
- Generate form map before content (sections, bars per section).
- Allow planned modulations (often into or in the bridge).
- Ensure harmonic rhythm supports lyric intensity (slower under emotional lines).
- Enforce natural word stress against melodic accents.
- Penalise cliché phrase templates; prefer concrete imagery tokens.
- Require at least one strong hook motif reused in multiple contexts.
- Use bridges as harmonic and emotional contrast.
- Keep line length and stanza design consistent within a song.

### Pat Pattison [web:64][web:71]

#### Core principles
- Prosody: everything must “say the same thing”.
- Stability vs instability in rhythm, harmony, and rhyme.

#### Melody rules
- Stable moments: long notes, stepwise motion.
- Unstable moments: faster notes, leaps.

#### Harmony / structure rules
- Align harmonic stability with lyric stability.
- Use contrast between verse and chorus intensity.

#### Lyric/prosody rules
- Match stressed syllables to strong beats.
- Use rhyme schemes and line lengths to signal stability/instability.

#### Practical techniques
- Object writing, sensory detail, “boxes” for rhyme and rhythm.

#### ENGINE RULES (Pat Pattison)
- Tag lyric lines as “stable” or “unstable” and map to stable/unstable music.
- Place stressed syllables on strong beats or long notes.
- In unstable sections (verses/conflict), use more syncopation and non‑cadential chords.
- In stable sections (chorus/resolution), use more root‑position tonics and regular phrasing.
- Implement line length consistency within section; contrast between sections.
- Use strong rhyme in stable sections, weaker rhyme in unstable sections.
- Ensure melodic peaks coincide with key emotional words.
- Structure pre‑chorus as increasing instability leading into chorus resolution.
- Apply object‑like detail tags in lyric generator (sensory words encouraged).

### Jack Perricone [web:62][web:71]

#### Core principles
- Melodic, harmonic, and rhythmic cells as building blocks.
- Hierarchy of motifs and structure.

#### Melody rules
- Distinct motif types (A, B, C) reused and varied.
- Use antecedent/consequent phrase pairs.

#### Harmony / structure rules
- Chord function categories: tonic, pre‑dominant, dominant.
- Clear cadential patterns and phrase endings.

#### Lyric/prosody rules
- Align phrase structure with lyrical syntactic units.

#### Practical techniques
- Map motif usage across form, ensure balance of repetition/contrast.

#### ENGINE RULES (Jack Perricone)
- Define motif types (A,B,C) with occurrence quotas per section.
- Require phrase pairs with slight variation (AA’, AB).
- Classify chords as T, PD, D and enforce logical sequences (T–PD–D–T).
- Generate cadences at end of phrases using D→T moves.
- Assign motif types to sections (e.g. A in verse, B in chorus).
- Maintain consistent phrase lengths within sections but allow asymmetry across song.
- Align lyrical clause boundaries with musical cadences.
- Vary rhythm and contour of motifs while preserving identity (interval/rhythm constraints).
- Use pre‑chorus for PD/D dominance leading into T‑weighted chorus.

## SECTION 4 — HOOK AND SONG ARCHITECTURE

### Concepts

- Strong hook: short, memorable unit (melodic/rhythmic/lyric/harmonic) repeated at structural points.
- Types: melodic contour, rhythmic pattern, distinctive chord move, striking phrase.

### ENGINE RULES (Hook + structure, 15–20)

1. Define at least one primary hook object (melodic, rhythmic, lyrical, or harmonic) per song.
2. Place primary hook in chorus and, optionally, intro and outro.
3. Ensure hook duration is short (0.5–2 bars).
4. Repeat hook at least 3–4 times across song.
5. For melodic hooks, keep interval content simple and contour distinct.
6. For rhythmic hooks, align to drums; avoid overly complex rhythms.
7. For harmonic hooks, use a distinctive progression segment (e.g. IV–iv–I, bVII–IV–I).
8. For lyrical hooks, centre on title; repeat title within hook.
9. Chorus: higher average pitch and intensity than verses.
10. Chorus harmony should feel more stable and grounded than verse.
11. Verses: more narrative, less hook repetition; build toward chorus.
12. Pre‑chorus: increase harmonic and rhythmic tension leading into chorus.
13. Bridges: introduce new harmony, melody contour, or point of view.
14. Ensure clear section boundaries via changes in melody, harmony, or texture.
15. Keep time‑to‑first‑hook under specified threshold (e.g. 45–60 seconds).
16. Allow different hook types to reinforce each other (same rhythm + lyric + contour).
17. Prevent hook fatigue by limiting consecutive repetitions (e.g. max 4 back‑to‑back).
18. Use breakdown or dynamic drop before final big hook repetition.

## SECTION 5 — MELODY SYSTEMS (CRITICAL)

### ENGINE RULES (15)

1. Represent melodies as sequences of motif objects plus variation rules.
2. Keep most intervals stepwise; reserve leaps for emotional peaks.
3. Limit overall vocal range by style (e.g. 9–12 semitones default).
4. Use contour archetypes (arch, ramp, inverted arch, wave).
5. Pair antecedent/consequent phrases (A, A’).
6. Apply rhythmic repetition with small variations (syncopation, pick‑ups).
7. Align phrase lengths with form (2, 4, 8 bars) but allow occasional asymmetry.
8. Place highest note of song in chorus or near emotional climax.
9. Use longer note values on structurally important words/beats.
10. Use chromatic notes as passing/neighbor tones or color, not random.
11. Support scale modes per style (major, minor, Dorian, Mixolydian, etc.).
12. Assign motif roles (hook vs filler) and treat them differently in variation.
13. Avoid constant high‑range writing; include contour rest points.
14. Ensure cadential notes are chord tones in functional harmony contexts.
15. In lyric‑driven modes, let syllable count drive rhythmic grouping.

## SECTION 6 — HARMONY IN SONGWRITING

### ENGINE RULES (15)

1. Tag each section as functional, modal, or non‑functional.
2. In functional sections, enforce tonic–pre‑dominant–dominant–tonic cycles.
3. Use diatonic triads and 7ths as base; add 9/11/13 according to style.
4. Provide style‑specific progression templates (I–V–vi–IV, ii–V–I, blues, etc.).
5. Allow modal sections with restricted root movement and fewer cadences.
6. For jazz‑leaning styles, enable extended/altered dominants and substitutions.
7. Use upper‑structure triads to encode specific tension sets.
8. Control harmonic rhythm via per‑section parameters (chords per bar).
9. Slow harmonic rhythm in emotional/high‑melodic sections to support vocals.
10. Speed harmonic rhythm in transitional or high‑energy passages.
11. Use pedal points for tension (bass fixed, chords move) as an option.
12. Keep modulations to section boundaries or climactic shifts.
13. Provide “surprise chord” slots (borrowed chords, bVII, bVI etc.) per song.
14. Ensure voice‑leading between chords avoids large inner‑voice jumps.
15. For non‑functional sections, prioritise color continuity (shared tones) over root motion rules.

## SECTION 7 — LYRIC + PROSODY (CRITICAL)

### ENGINE RULES (12–15)

1. Mark stressed syllables in generated text and align them to strong beats/long notes.
2. Avoid placing weak grammatical words (the, and, of) on the highest/longest notes.
3. Match line length (syllable count) to melodic phrase length, within set tolerances.
4. Use consistent line lengths and rhyme scheme per section (e.g. ABAB).
5. Contrast rhyme strength between sections (weaker in verse, stronger in chorus) for prosodic clarity. [web:64]
6. Ensure key emotional words occur on melodic peaks and/or harmonic cadences.
7. Avoid over‑crowding syllables on fast melismas unless style demands it.
8. Map sentence boundaries to phrase boundaries where possible.
9. Use imagery‑heavy words in high‑impact positions; generic words in passing positions.
10. Allow conversational looseness in verse; tighten symmetry in chorus.
11. Keep pronoun and narrative perspective consistent unless intentionally changed in bridge.
12. Provide possibilities for refrain lines that repeat verbatim each verse.
13. In rhythm‑first songs, adjust lyric rhythm to match pre‑existing groove patterns.
14. Penalise forced rhymes and unnatural word order where detectable.

## SECTION 9 — MINIMUM VIABLE SONGWRITING ENGINE

### Core rules (must‑have)

- Represent songs as section graphs (verse, chorus, bridge, etc.) with duration and order parameters.
- Implement melody engine with motif objects, contour types, and variation rules.
- Implement harmony engine with section type (functional/modal) and progression templates.
- Implement lyric engine with syllable, stress, rhyme, and semantic tags.
- Implement prosody mapping to align lyric stress with melodic and harmonic emphasis.
- Implement hook objects and ensure repeated placement in key structural positions.
- Implement basic style presets (e.g. Bacharach, Beatles, Dylan, Max Martin) toggling default rules.

### Style rules (songwriter‑driven)

- Each songwriter preset sets:
  - Preferred form templates.
  - Harmony vocabulary (diatonic vs extended vs modal).
  - Melodic range and interval profile.
  - Rhythm density and syncopation.
  - Lyric density and prosody looseness.
  - Hook placement and type priority.

### Advanced rules (later expansion)

- Player/performer nuance models (behind‑beat phrasing, vibrato, etc.).
- Multi‑song project coherence (motifs across songs, key relationships).
- Adaptive lyric semantics (topic modeling, character voice).
- Production‑aware choices (register to leave space for arrangements).
- Cross‑influence presets (e.g. “Beatles + Bacharach”, “Wonder + Martin”).
